Recovery protocols for athletes and trainees

Sleep
- 7–9 hours per night. Most important recovery lever.
- Keep schedule consistent. Dark, cool room. Limit screens before bed.

Nutrition post-workout
- Within 1–2 hours: protein (20–40 g) and carbs (0.5–1 g per kg) to replenish glycogen and support muscle repair.
- Example: chicken and rice, Greek yogurt and fruit, or a shake with banana.

Hydration
- Replace fluids lost in training. Weigh before/after to estimate sweat loss.
- Electrolytes if training long or in heat (sodium, potassium, magnesium).

Active recovery
- Light movement on rest days: walking, cycling, swimming, yoga. Promotes blood flow without adding stress.

Stretching and mobility
- Dynamic before training; static or PNF after. Focus on tight areas (hips, thoracic spine, hamstrings).

Foam rolling and massage
- Can reduce perceived soreness. Use on major muscle groups; avoid joints and bones.

Contrast therapy (optional)
- Alternating hot/cold (e.g. sauna then cold shower) may support circulation and perceived recovery. Evidence is mixed; use if it feels good.

Deload weeks
- Every 4–6 weeks reduce volume (sets/reps) or load by ~40% for one week to allow adaptation and reduce injury risk.

After heavy leg day
- Extra protein and carbs. Light walk or bike next day. Stretch quads, hamstrings, hip flexors. Stay hydrated.

After upper body / strength
- Protein and sleep. Mobilize shoulders and thoracic spine. Optional foam roll on upper back.

Stress management
- High life stress impairs recovery. Sleep, nutrition, and manageable training load come first; add techniques like breathing or meditation if helpful.
